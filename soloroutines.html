Equipment & Space Requirements

Do I need any special equipment to perform these routines?
Besides a standard squash racket and suitable balls (See my Use The Right Ball guide), no special equipment is required. However, having a few extra balls, with one held in your non-racket hand to keep it warm) can help maintain momentum, and using A4 paper targets is occasionally recommended.

How much court time should I book for these routines?
I recommend booking a 45-minute slot for each 30-40 minute routine. This gives you time to heat up and cool down properly.

Can I practice these routines on a racquetball or other similar court?
Yes, I suppose you could, but obviously it's better to use a proper squash court.

Skill Level & Progression

What makes the Solo Routines programme different from other squash training programmes?
The Solo Routines programme is uniquely structured around themed routines and focused drills that are designed to improve specific aspects of your game. Each routine comes with a detailed PDF guide sheet, an explainer video, and access to the Routine Timer app, ensuring you get the most out of every practice session.

Can I use these routines if I’m a complete beginner?
I don't advise using the Club Players: Set One routine. As the name suggests, it's designed for players who have been playing at least a year. However, some beginners have purchased Set One and reported that they benefited from it. Sometime in 2025, I will be releasing a Beginners set, but I have no release date for that.

I'm returning to squash after years away. Is this programme suitable for me?
Yes, especially if you had previous experience. The Club Players: Set 1 can help you rebuild your skills methodically. Focus on quality over quantity as you regain your touch.

How quickly should I expect to see improvement?
Most players notice improvements in their ball control within 2-3 weeks of consistent practice (2-3 sessions per week). However, significant improvements typically take 6-8 weeks of regular practice.

How long does each routine typically last?
Between 30 to 40 minutes.

How often should I practice each routine?
Honestly, that's a tough one to answer. Ideally between 5 and 10 times per month.

Can I mix routines in a single training session?
Yes, players should feel free to adapt and adjust their training to suit THEIR needs.

Do the routines focus on specific skills or overall improvement?
Each routine is themed and generally is focused on certain aspects of ball control.

Programme Implementation

How often should I practice these routines?
For optimal results, we recommend 1-2 solo practice sessions per week. This frequency allows for skill development while preventing burnout.

Should I repeat the same routine multiple times or rotate through all 12?
I recommend rotating through all 12 routines initially to build a broad foundation. Once you identify specific areas needing improvement, you can focus more on those particular routines.

Can I modify the routines to match my specific weaknesses?
Absolutely! While the routines are designed to be comprehensive, feel free to extend sections that address your weaknesses or modify drills to target specific aspects of your game.

Using Solo Routines with Others

Can I use these routines as part of a coaching session with my students?
For coaching purposes, you should purchase the Commercial license, which permits sharing the routines with students in your facility.

Can I practice these routines with a partner?
While designed as solo routines, many drills can be adapted for partner practice. The Discord community is a great place to ask about partner adaptations for specific routines.

A Commercial license allows you to post the routine on your noticeboard and make a reasonable number of photocopies to distribute WITHIN your facility. Consider posting a "Routine of the Month" on your noticeboard and organising designated solo practice sessions where members can work on the routines together but individually.

My Approach to Solo Drilling

How I structure the sessions.

Keeping your mind engaged is essential when practicing solo drills. That’s why most of my drills last less than three minutes and incorporate the Set and Mega-Set concepts.

Drill repetition is also key—it ensures you spend enough time on each drill without it becoming monotonous.

Throughout the year, I adjust the difficulty, type, focus, and duration of drills. During summer, foundational drills take priority, while in-season training includes more aggressive and kill-oriented drills.

Solo Drilling Guide

Start with the right mindset.

➤ Have a plan. Luckily, you joined my programme, so that's taken care of. But it is also a good idea to read and understand the routine BEFORE you go onto the court. I'm not saying memorise it, but at least have read it first.

➤ Always heat up properly before you start drilling. By taking the Solo Drills seriously you will maximise your time and train yourself to always focus on court.

➤ Hold a spare ball of the same dot colour and condition as the ball you are using in your non-hitting hand. This will ensure minimum disruption if you break a ball during the routine.

➤ Using targets is a great way to focus your mind. But not everybody benefits from them. Some players find that they become worried when they don't hit them. Over the year, I will add targets for appropriate drills, but they will always be optional.

➤ Use which ever routine feels suitable for you that training session. You do not have to use the same one for the whole month.

➤ I would recommend a minimum of one solo training session per week and up to four, but you will need to adjust that to suit your needs and objectives.

➤ Many solo drills can be performed with a partner sharing the court, so I recommend finding a training partner to share the cost of the court. Just don't be tempted to play games instead.
